About

Every year Texas Engineering World Health develops medical projects for the developing world. We work towards submitting our projects to the Engineering World Health Competition. We research the prevalent problems that need solutions and we look into making the idea financially feasible for hospitals in the developing world. We aim to create a prototype that encompasses engineering skills from all different engineering fields.

This year, we are working on four different projects:
1] Cardiac Detection,
2] Leukemia Detection

Ultrasonic Vision for the Blind

Ultrasonic Vision for the Blind

90% of the world's blind and visually impaired live in developing countries. Although canes are a common solution, learning to navigate with a cane takes 3-12 months of training. Because of this, we intend to develop a device consisting of ultrasonic sensors placed on various body parts to allow greater mobility for visually impaired individuals.

Malaria Detection

According to UNICEF, over one million people die from malaria each year, mostly children under five years of age. Test-based diagnosis in developing countries is unpopular due to its relatively high cost. We will use machine learning to predict malaria more effectively based on image data from blood smears.

Solar Autoclave

Most hospitals in developing countries don't have access to a constant supply of electricity. Autoclaves use pressure and heat to sterilized medical tools, but without a constant supply of electricity, there is no sustainable way to ensure medical tools are kept sterile. We will create a collapsible autoclave that focuses sunlight for reliable sterilization.

FreePulse

FreePulse is a low-cost patient monitor that provides basic monitoring for heart rate, electrocardiogram signals, and oxygen saturation. This project was the winner of the 2015 Engineering World Health design competition and was the third place winner in the 2015 NIH DEBUT competition.

Neonatal Bili Light Incubator for Juandice

Blue bili lights in the incubator break down bilirubin in the blood to treat neonatal jaundice. The incubator is fully functional and supplies a constant body temperature and airflow to the newborn.

Child Vitals Monitor

Last year, we created a child vitals monitor, with probes for oxygen saturation, blood pressure, heart rate, and body temperature. Data is sent to an Android app via Bluetooth.
